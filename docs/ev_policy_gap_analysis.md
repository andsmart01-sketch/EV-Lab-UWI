# EV Policy Gap Analysis
## Jamaica National Electric Vehicle Policy (June 2023)
**Prepared by:** Andrew Smart, EV Lab Research Project, UWI Mona
**Date:** June 19, 2026
**Supervisor:** Dr. Louis-Ray Harris

---

## 1. Policy Overview

The Government of Jamaica published its National Electric Vehicle Policy in June 2023, led by the
Ministry of Science, Energy, Telecommunications and Transport (MSETT). The policy establishes
six goals and a three-phased implementation strategy. Its headline targets, drawn from the
Strategic Framework for Electric Mobility, are:

- 12% of privately owned vehicle fleet to be EVs by 2030
- 16% of public transport fleet to be EVs by 2030
- 100% of Government of Jamaica (GOJ) fleet to be EVs by 2030

The policy committed to a Policy Implementation Plan to be finalised within 90 days of approval
(approximately September 2023). As of June 2026, no publicly available version of that
Implementation Plan has been located. This is a primary gap.

---

## 2. Six Policy Goals: Summary and Assessment

### Goal 1: Enabling Environment for EV Fleet Penetration

**What the policy says:**
Establish EV import technical standards, update vehicle registration procedures for EVs,
develop a battery labelling system, publish import guidelines in collaboration with Jamaica
Customs Agency, and promote alternatives for public and government fleets.

**Responsible agencies:** MSETT, TBL (Trade Board of Jamaica), Jamaica Customs Agency, MIIC, NEPA

**Timelines set in policy:**
- Import technical requirements: under 6 months
- Battery labelling system: 1 year
- Import guidelines publication: under 6 months
- Update of inspection procedures: 1 year

**Assessment (as of June 2026):**
These timelines ran from June 2023, meaning the 6-month and 1-year deadlines fell in
December 2023 and June 2024 respectively. No public documentation confirming the
completion of EV-specific import standards, a battery labelling system, or revised Customs
procedures has been located. The Trade Board of Jamaica website does not list EV-specific
import categories as of the date of this analysis.

**Gap:** No evidence of a completed battery labelling system or published EV import guidelines
three years after the policy's release. Registration procedures for EVs at the Tax Administration
Jamaica (TAJ) appear to follow ICE vehicle procedures with no EV-specific field categories.

---

### Goal 2: Charging Infrastructure Deployment

**What the policy says:**
Develop a national EV charging plan, enhance grid readiness (in collaboration with JPS and OUR),
engage private stakeholders (e.g. Evergo) for public charging deployment, set technical, safety,
and accessibility standards for EVSE (Electric Vehicle Supply Equipment), and make charging
location information publicly available.

**Responsible agencies:** MSETT, JPS, OUR, local authorities

**Timelines set in policy:**
- Planning strategy: short-term (1-3 years from approval)
- Grid readiness assessment: short-term
- Standards publication: short-term

**Known baseline at time of writing (as stated in the policy itself):**
25 public charging points existed in Jamaica as of March 2022.

**Assessment (as of June 2026):**
Evergo operates the largest public network and has expanded since 2022. Exact current count
is unconfirmed pending Evergo's response to data request (sent June 10, 2026). No published
national charging location database has been found. JPS has not published a grid readiness
assessment or dedicated EV tariff structure as of this writing.

**Gap:** The national charging plan (the central commitment of Goal 2) has not been publicly
released. No OUR decision on EVSE market regulation has been publicly located. The policy
committed to a "publicly available charging infrastructure information" platform; this does not
appear to exist in a centralised form.

---

### Goal 3: Competitive EV Infrastructure Market

**What the policy says:**
Develop operating guidelines for charging service providers, establish interoperability standards
(so any EV can use any charger regardless of network), and promote open communication
protocols.

**Responsible agencies:** MSETT, BSJ (Bureau of Standards Jamaica), OUR

**Timelines:** Short to medium-term (1-5 years)

**Assessment (as of June 2026):**
No published interoperability standard for Jamaican EVSE has been located. The current market
appears to have at least two private networks (Evergo and Flash Motors-affiliated stations)
operating independently. Whether their chargers use compatible protocols is not confirmed by
any published source.

**Gap:** Without a mandated interoperability standard, EV owners may face network lock-in
where their vehicle's connector type or network subscription restricts access. This directly
undermines public adoption. No evidence that BSJ has issued an EV-specific standard.

---

### Goal 4: Battery End-of-Life Management

**What the policy says:**
Analyse and outline strategies for battery re-use and repurposing; develop recycling and
disposal guidelines; establish Authorised Treatment Facilities (ATFs); collaborate with NSWMA
(National Solid Waste Management Authority) on hazardous waste handling; remove batteries
prior to vehicle disposal.

**Responsible agencies:** MSETT, NEPA, NSWMA, MEGJC

**Timelines set in policy:**
- Battery collection and transport regulation: 1-3 years
- Battery status assessment system: 3-5 years
- Re-use guidelines: 1-3 years
- Recycling procedures: 1-3 years
- ATF establishment: 1-3 years

**Assessment (as of June 2026):**
The 1-3 year timelines from June 2023 run through June 2024 to June 2026. No ATF certified
specifically for EV batteries has been publicly identified in Jamaica. NSWMA's published mandate
covers general solid waste and does not list EV battery handling as a current programme.
The Green Paper on Hazardous Garbage Management referenced in the policy was submitted
to Cabinet in draft form prior to 2023; its status post-approval of the EV policy is not confirmed.

**Gap: This is the most serious gap in the entire policy.**

Reasons:
1. No currently operating ATF for EV batteries means that as the EV fleet grows (even slowly),
   there is no certified disposal pathway for end-of-life battery packs.
2. The policy acknowledges batteries are classified as hazardous waste, yet no published
   hazardous materials transport regulation specific to EV batteries exists.
3. Battery assessment infrastructure (testing whether a pack can be repurposed) requires
   technical equipment and certified personnel; no training programme for this has been
   publicly announced by MSETT or HEART/NSTA.
4. The policy does not set a minimum recovery or recycling rate target. Many jurisdictions
   (e.g. EU Battery Regulation 2023) mandate specific recovery percentages by weight;
   Jamaica's policy contains no such quantified obligation.
5. There is no producer responsibility mechanism. The policy does not require vehicle
   importers or battery manufacturers to fund or manage end-of-life recovery.

---

### Goal 5: Training and Workforce Development

**What the policy says:**
Develop certification programmes for EV mechanics, technicians, first responders (police,
fire), and EVSE electricians; establish Skill Centers; promote university research on battery
re-use.

**Responsible agencies:** MSETT, MEGJC, MOEY, Universities (including UWI)

**Timelines:**
- Certification programmes for mechanics and technicians: under 6 months
- First responder programmes: 2-3 years
- Skill Centers: 1-3 years

**Assessment (as of June 2026):**
The 6-month deadline for initial certification programmes ran to December 2023. No publicly
available EV technician certification from HEART/NSTA or MSETT has been located. UWI's
published course catalogues (Mona and St. Augustine) do not list dedicated EV or battery
technology programmes as of this writing.

**Gap:** Without certified EV mechanics, the maintenance cost advantage of EVs is partially
offset by the risk of unqualified servicing. First responders handling EV accidents (which
involve different hazards from ICE fires, particularly lithium battery thermal runaway) require
specific training that does not appear to be in place.

---

### Goal 6: Public Awareness and Incentives

**What the policy says:**
Launch communication campaigns, provide EV driving experiences for the public, implement
parking incentives for EVs, and explore Low Emission Zones (LEZs).

**Responsible agencies:** MSETT, MFPS, MOEY, Universities

**Timelines:**
- Parking incentives: under 6 months (by December 2023)
- Low Emission Zone: 1 year (by June 2024)
- Communication campaigns: 1-3 years

**Assessment (as of June 2026):**
Dedicated EV parking bays exist at some private locations (e.g. some malls where Evergo
chargers are installed), but no national policy on reserved public parking for EVs appears to
have been implemented. No Low Emission Zone has been designated in Kingston or any other
Jamaican city. No structured national communication campaign has been publicly identified,
beyond the policy itself.

**Gap:** The LEZ commitment was the most concrete urban planning action in the policy and
it missed its 1-year deadline by at least a year as of this writing. LEZs are the primary tool
used globally to create a market signal for fleet electrification in dense urban areas.

## 3. Structural Gaps Not Addressed by Any Goal

The following issues are absent from the policy or inadequately addressed across all six goals:

| Gap | Explanation |
|-----|-------------|
| No quantified charging coverage target | The policy does not specify how many public chargers Jamaica needs by 2030, where they should be located, or at what density. Without this, deployment is directionless. |
| No used EV import framework | A significant share of Jamaica's vehicle market is used imports. The policy addresses new EV imports but does not address the specific condition, warranty, and battery health requirements for used EV imports, which is where most Jamaicans will actually access EVs. |
| No grid emissions disclosure requirement | The policy links EVs to environmental benefit but does not require JPS to publish a verified grid emissions intensity figure (kgCO2/kWh). Without this, EV environmental claims cannot be independently validated. |
| No solar PV-EV integration framework | The policy does not address home or commercial solar charging, despite Jamaica's solar resource and falling PV costs. This is a missed co-benefit. |
| No producer responsibility scheme | No obligation on vehicle importers or battery manufacturers to fund end-of-life battery collection. This leaves the cost burden undefined. |
| No public fleet procurement timeline | The 100% GOJ fleet electrification target by 2030 is stated but no procurement schedule, budget allocation, or responsible ministry is named. |
| No monitoring dashboard or public reporting mechanism | The policy commits to monitoring and evaluation but no public reporting mechanism (annual report, online tracker) has been established to let civil society or researchers assess progress. |
| No explicit EV incentive sunset provision | Import duty concessions and other incentives are referenced but the policy does not define when they expire, which creates investor uncertainty. |

## 5. End-of-Life Battery Policy: Detailed Assessment

The policy devotes Goal 4 entirely to battery management. Despite this, the framework has
four core weaknesses:

**1. No binding producer responsibility.**
The policy assigns collection and recycling duties to MSETT, NEPA, and NSWMA but does
not obligate battery importers or vehicle dealers to fund or manage recovery. In the EU
and UK, Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) schemes place the cost burden on producers.
Jamaica's policy is silent on who pays.

**2. No quantified recovery target.**
The EU Battery Regulation (2023) mandates 50% recovery of lithium by weight by 2027,
rising to 80% by 2031. Jamaica's policy states intent to develop recycling procedures but
sets no recovery percentage or weight-based target.

**3. No import restriction on used batteries with degraded state-of-health.**
Jamaica imports used vehicles. A used EV arriving with a battery at 60% state-of-health
has a shorter remaining life and reaches end-of-life sooner. The policy does not set a
minimum battery health threshold for imported used EVs, creating a potential wave of
early-retirement batteries with no disposal infrastructure to receive them.

**4. ATF infrastructure is aspirational, not funded.**
The policy calls for establishing ATFs in the 1-3 year window from June 2023. This requires
physical facilities, specialised equipment, and trained personnel. No budget line, no site
selection, and no construction or licensing timeline has been publicly announced.
